Suggested title: "PSA: You do not need an employer (or the H-1B lottery) to get a US green card. How EB-2 NIW actually works."

Post:

I see a version of this question weekly: "My employer will not sponsor me, is it over?" Short answer: no. There are two green card categories you can file for yourself, with no employer involvement: EB-1A and EB-2 NIW. NIW is the more accessible one for most people, so here is how it actually works.

NIW is judged under a 2016 case called Matter of Dhanasar, which sets 3 prongs:

1. Your proposed work has substantial merit and national importance. This does not mean curing cancer. Work in areas like public health, AI safety, semiconductors, energy, education research, and many others has qualified. You need to articulate why it matters beyond your employer.

2. You are well positioned to advance it. This is about evidence: your degree, publications, citations, patents, work history, letters from experts who can speak to your record, and a concrete plan.

3. On balance, it benefits the US to waive the usual job offer and labor certification requirement. Often argued from urgency of the field or the impracticality of tying the work to a single employer.

Things people get wrong:

You do not need to be famous. You need documented evidence on each prong. A PhD student with 2 or 3 strong publications and a clear research agenda can have a real case. So can an engineer whose work has measurable impact beyond their company.

Approval is discretionary. USCIS reiterated this in a May 21, 2026 policy memo. Nobody can promise you an approval, and you should run from anyone who does. What you can control is the strength and documentation of your case.

Start early. Evidence takes months to build (judging, letters, impact documentation), and with the proposed rule that would cap F-1 admission at a fixed period now in final review, open-ended timelines are not something I would count on.

Talk to a licensed immigration attorney before filing anything. This is strategy and education, not legal advice.
