## 4. Reddit

Target subreddits: r/USCIS, r/f1visa (check each sub's self-promo rules before including any link or mention)

**Suggested post/comment (fits threads asking "Can a regular PhD student / early-career person actually get EB-1A or NIW?"):**

Short answer: yes, and the people who succeed usually do not look superhuman on paper when they start. Here is the honest version.

EB-1A is evaluated under the Kazarian two-step. Step one is mechanical: do you meet at least 3 of the 10 listed criteria (judging, publications, original contributions, press, awards, etc.)? Step two is the final merits determination, where the officer weighs whether the totality of evidence shows sustained acclaim. Most DIY filers fail step two because they treat 3 criteria as a checkbox instead of building a coherent story of impact.

NIW is the Dhanasar three-prong test: (1) your work has substantial merit and national importance, (2) you are well positioned to advance it, and (3) on balance, waiving the job offer requirement benefits the US. For students and early-career researchers, prong 2 is usually the battleground. Citations, adoption of your work, letters from independent experts, and a concrete track record matter most.

Practical advice that applies to almost everyone here:

Start documenting now, not when you decide to file. Review for journals when invited (that is the judging criterion building itself). Keep evidence of anyone independent using or citing your work. The standard of proof is preponderance of the evidence (Chawathe), more likely than not, not beyond doubt. People talk themselves out of filing because they imagine a higher bar than the law actually sets.

One more timing note: the rule that would replace D/S with a fixed 4-year F-1 admission is in final federal review (not in effect yet). If it lands, planning ahead becomes even more valuable.
