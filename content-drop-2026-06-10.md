**Post/comment:**

A lot of people here ask "do I qualify for NIW?" so here's the actual legal test, because it's shorter and more concrete than most people expect.

Every NIW is decided under Matter of Dhanasar (2016), a 3-prong test:

1. Substantial merit and national importance. About your work's field and impact, not your job title or salary. STEM research, public health, critical tech, and education work all routinely qualify on this prong.

2. Well positioned to advance the endeavor. The decisive prong in most cases. Officers want evidence: publications, citations, patents, funded projects, independent expert letters, and a record showing momentum. A PhD helps but is not required. Fame is not required anywhere in the test.

3. On balance, beneficial to waive the job offer and labor certification. You're arguing the US gains more by letting you self-petition than by forcing you through employer sponsorship (PERM).

Two practical notes for 2026:

- USCIS issued a memo on May 21 (PM-602-0199) reaffirming that adjustment of status is discretionary. It doesn't change NIW eligibility, but it does mean sloppy or thin filings carry more risk. Build the record first, file second.
- Don't self-reject. The most common mistake I see is strong candidates assuming "NIW is for famous people." It's an evidence test, not a fame test. The second most common mistake is filing a thin case too early.
